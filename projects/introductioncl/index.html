Introduction to Computational Linguistics

January 2018 · 2 min

The ambition to create artificial being, mechanical, that were able to act as humans do, has always been in our fantasy. Computer Science is defined as the discipline that deals with the collection and process of information (De Mauro, 2000), and since linguistics handle the analysis and study of the most powerful tool humans have to express information (the language), their match was unavoidable.

The possibility to build machines able to produce language is based on the existence of a model, that can either be different from the one used by humans or can shed a light on it.

There are two main approaches. The first define a linguistic rule as the description of a practice, i. e. a probabilistic and statistic tendency in the behaviour. This is the so-called statistic-probabilistic, largely based on data extracted from real texts and with the aim of imitation.

Noam Chomsky on the other side proposes a rule-based approach with the idea of a syntactic innate rule set, being part of the linguistic competence, that from a finite and abstract set of rules (two base features of programming languages) is able to allow the build of an infinite well formed set of sentences (Chomsky, 1957). This is the so-called rule-based model.

At first sight, the rule-based approach looks the easiest one, since the limited number of rules to consider, but in the language there exist countless factors that are against us (?).

These factors are the infinite number of new sentences and words, the existence of synonyms, polysemys, omonymys, multiword expressions, collocations, the different meaning of a word, the difference between the meaning of the various translations of a word in another language, the extending of the meaning of a word, the syntactic ambiguity, the anaphoric and cataphoric references and sometimes even quantifiers. A big importance is covered by metalinguistics, that is able to give grammatical sense to standard ungrammatical structures. And of course pragmatics as well. And of course it’s important to identify all the rules to implement, a much more difficult job than expected!

The paper was written for the course “Applied Linguistics” in my Language, Civilisation and the Science of Language Bachelor at Ca’ Foscari University of Venice.
